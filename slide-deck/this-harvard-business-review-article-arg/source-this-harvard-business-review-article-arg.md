This Harvard Business Review article argues that breakthrough innovations often fail to scale because organizations lack "bridgers"—leaders who excel at collaborating across organizational boundaries.

The Core Problem
Modern innovation increasingly requires partnerships across teams, companies, and sectors, but these collaborations frequently break down due to diverging priorities, capabilities, and constraints. Different groups speak different languages: product teams prioritize experimentation, compliance focuses on regulations, IT emphasizes operational reliability, and executives demand business cases. Traditional solutions like project managers, cross-functional teams, and formal governance structures fail because they don't create the trust necessary for the risk-taking inherent in innovation.

What Bridgers Do
Bridgers perform three critical, overlapping functions:

Curating partners: They build diverse networks, identify stakeholders with needed capabilities or authority, and earn trust through deep listening and empathy. For example, Raja Al Mazrouei at Dubai International Financial Centre brought together risk-averse financial institutions, fast-moving startups, and cautious regulators by understanding each group's priorities and concerns.

Translating among partners: They expose differences through dialogue rather than minimizing them, using strategic storytelling to make technical concepts accessible. Garry Lyons at Mastercard Labs used physical prototypes and personalized one-on-one conversations to help non-technical executives understand emerging technologies like blockchain and tokenization.

Integrating disparate efforts: They facilitate co-creation of shared operating models, decision criteria, and metrics while articulating a compelling "north star" that keeps partners aligned. Nicole M. Jones at Delta Air Lines developed an "Initiative Canvas" that forced partners to agree on fundamentals upfront, dramatically accelerating later decision-making.

Required Skills
Bridgers need two intelligence types:

Emotional intelligence: Self-awareness, humility, empathy, and the ability to manage emotions while navigating ambiguity without direct control

Contextual intelligence: Understanding how each partner's metrics, culture, power dynamics, and unspoken rules shape their behavior, and anticipating resistance signals

Developing Bridgers
Organizations should identify potential bridgers by looking for people who already work successfully at boundaries and build robust networks. Development strategies include zigzag career paths, cross-functional rotations, stretch assignments, and involvement in external communities. Leaders must provide air cover, give bridgers visibility, and deliberately reward collective achievements to prevent burnout. The article notes that forward-deployed engineers bridging IT and business teams during AI deployments have become "the hottest job in tech".
