The article argues that generative AI can significantly boost novices’ performance and speed, but it cannot turn true nonexperts into experts or fully replace deep domain expertise.

Core findings
Researchers ran a controlled experiment at UK fintech firm IG Group with three groups: expert writers, adjacent outsiders (marketers), and distant outsiders (developers/data scientists).

All three groups used gen AI to conceptualize and write website-style articles, with some participants randomly assigned AI assistance and others not.

For idea generation, gen AI raised all groups’ scores and largely closed the gap between experts and nonexperts; marketers using AI even slightly outperformed writers using AI, and all AI-assisted groups beat writers without AI.

For actual article writing, gen AI helped, but nonexperts still could not consistently match experts’ quality; technologists’ scores barely changed with AI.

The “Gen AI wall”
The study introduces the “AI wall”: the further someone is from the relevant knowledge domain, the less AI can help them reach expert-level performance.

Conceptualizing topics demands less deep expertise, so AI can help outsiders more; writing requires nuanced domain judgment and language, which nonexperts lack.

Adjacent outsiders (marketers) could refine AI outputs because they understood marketing language and goals, while distant outsiders (technologists) often copy-pasted AI text without knowing what to keep or change.

Implications for organizations
The findings challenge the idea that gen AI can flatten skill hierarchies and enable “universal task fluidity”; AI’s impact depends on the expertise distance between the user and the task.

Recommended practices:

Do not overestimate gen AI: users still need enough foundational knowledge to assess and improve AI outputs; AI is not a one-size-fits-all fix when tasks cannot be fully automated.

Rethink work design: to capture AI’s value, firms may need to adjust processes, decision rights, and team structures; roles in related domains (e.g., SEO specialist vs content strategist) may blur, but bridging larger gaps (e.g., across marketing, sales, product) is much harder.

Structural and cultural changes, plus attention to who is using AI and what they know, are critical because expertise remains irreplaceable.

Practitioner perspective from IG Group
Former IG Group data and AI transformation head Olga Pirog says gen AI gave marketers the execution skills they lacked, letting them write almost as well as expert writers because they already knew what good marketing content looks like.

She argues gen AI shortens the journey to expertise by producing solid first drafts, freeing experts to focus on refinement, tone, and SEO rather than initial drafting.

However, AI only closes the gap for adjacent roles; for distant roles like technologists without marketing context, the performance gap stays wide because they cannot reliably judge AI outputs.

Pirog warns that over-relying on experts “editing AI” and cutting junior hiring risks destroying the pipeline for future experts, since taste and judgment require doing the work.

She suggests shifting apprenticeship and training toward teaching foundational concepts (what makes writing good) rather than just tactical execution, since AI can handle the grind once people have the right context.
